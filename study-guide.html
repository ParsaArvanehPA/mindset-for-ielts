Lead-In: Articles with Geographical Features

Understanding when to use the with place names

Read this paragraph about Mallorca and notice the article usage:

Passage

Mallorca, the biggest of the Balearic Islands, is situated in the Mediterranean Sea, some 300km from the Valencian coast of Spain, and is an exciting mix of urban energy, rural adventure and coastal calm. Although it isn't popular with the Spanish as a tourist destination, it annually welcomes people from all over Europe (particularly from the United Kingdom, France and Germany), who may head for Alcudia Beach, or go hiking in the Tramuntanas, a mountain range that runs from the northeast to the southwest of the island.

Skill Focus: Matching Headings

How to identify the main idea and choose the correct heading for each paragraph
⚠️
What is this task?
You must choose a heading that correctly summarises each paragraph. Often, one sentence in a paragraph conveys the main idea — this is the topic sentence. It can appear at the start, middle, or end.

Strategy: 5-Step Approach

Step 1: Read all the headings first and underline key words
Step 2: Read the first & last sentence of each paragraph (likely topic sentences)
Step 3: Match obvious paragraphs first to narrow down options
Step 4: For remaining paragraphs, read fully and identify the main idea
Step 5: Pay attention to adjectives & adverbs — they can eliminate wrong answers
T

Exam Tip: The correct heading will paraphrase the main idea — it won't use the exact same words as the paragraph. Don't be tricked by headings that repeat specific words from the text.

Practice: The World's Most Unusual Towns and Cities

01

Auroville, India

In today's world of conflict, greed and constant struggles for power, Auroville – aka 'the City of Dawn' – claims on its website that it was planned and built to create the ultimate model of unity, peace and harmony that can be projected across all humanity. It has no government, no one owns any property, and money rarely, if ever, changes hands. There is no leader and rules do not exist. While most experiments at creating the perfect city do not meet with success, the majority of Auroville's residents believe their city to be an exception. Although its critics point to the fact that levels of crime have been creeping up for some years now, its citizens choose to remain there, still believing in its utopian dream, still following its path towards a better world.

Which heading best fits this paragraph?

A The reason why attempts to create the perfect city always fail
B An urban ideal designed for an imperfect world
C A conflict between reality and imitation

Answer: B — "An urban ideal designed for an imperfect world." The paragraph describes Auroville as a utopian city (an "ideal") created to counter the problems of the real world ("imperfect world"). The heading paraphrases this idea without using the same words.

02

Longyearbyen, Norway

Longyearbyen, Spitsbergen, in the Svalbard Archipelago of Norway, holds the record for being the furthest north city in the world, boasting the world's most northerly school, airport and university. But what really sets it apart is that it can also lay claim to some of the world's strangest rules. In Longyearbyen, for example, it has been forbidden to die since 1950, the year in which scientists discovered that bodies simply cannot decompose there – the cold is too extreme. To this day, anyone found ill or dying is not given the chance to pass away, and is instead immediately taken by airplane or ship to another part of Norway so that they can die and be buried safely. But it doesn't end there. Aside from prohibiting death, there are more peculiar rules and freedoms in Longyearbyen. Residents are permitted to openly walk the streets with high-powered guns (there are 3,000 polar bears living locally). At the same time, no one is allowed to own any cats, which are forbidden because they are a danger to the bird population.

Which heading best fits?

A An unwelcoming place to die
B A city at the top of the world
C An unusual approach to regulation

Answer: C — "An unusual approach to regulation." While the first sentence suggests B ("top of the world"), the overall main idea is about the city's strange rules (regulations). Be careful: the first sentence can be misleading — always read the whole paragraph!

03

Marloth Park, South Africa

Despite the fact that the town of Marloth Park is close to the Kruger National Park, one of the largest game reserves in Africa, and despite the constant threat of visits from wild animals such as lions and hippopotamuses, its anxious residents are not allowed to build fences around their houses to keep out their neighbours.

In fact the only fence permitted in the town was built by the local authority, interestingly, with the aim of keeping humans out of the park, rather than containing the animals inside. Consequently, it is not unusual to see giraffes or elephants causing traffic jams, and even predator attacks on humans are unnervingly common – a lion was recently said to have mauled and eaten an escaping burglar. Yet even after this, while some residents then called for all lions to be rounded up and shot, others suggested that they be allowed to walk the thoroughfares as a type of crime control, after an increase in the number of burglaries. Everywhere in Marloth Park, a wary understanding exists between man and beast.

Which heading best fits?

A An unusual approach to regulation
B Dealing with the occasional dangers of the wild
C Where humans and animals cautiously co-exist

Answer: C — "Where humans and animals cautiously co-exist." Focus on the adverbs: the dangers are not "occasional" (B is wrong — they are constant). The main idea is the co-existence, and "cautiously" fits the "wary understanding" described in the text.

04

Hallstatt, Austria & China

The real Hallstatt is in Austria and is proud to be a traditional UNESCO* World Heritage Site. The Chinese Hallstatt is a carbon copy, built in Guangdong province, China, by a millionaire who sponsored the construction of the imitation town. It cost approximately $940 million to build, and looks exactly like the real Hallstatt, all the way down to its wooden houses, its narrow streets, and its funicular railway. When the residents of Austria's Hallstatt (including the mayor) were invited to visit it, they expressed pride that their town was considered so improbably beautiful that it had been reproduced in its entirety, but they still had cause for complaint. Originally, the Chinese company had promised to meet with the Austrian residents to confirm that they were happy for their homes to be copied; instead, they simply sent their employees to Austria to take photos, and they returned home to China without speaking to a single resident of the original Hallstatt.

* United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization

Which heading best fits?

A A conflict between reality and imitation
B The importance of official recognition
C The result of encouraging wider investment

Answer: A — "A conflict between reality and imitation." The paragraph is about the tension between the real Austrian Hallstatt and the Chinese copy. The "conflict" refers to the residents' mixed feelings (pride vs. complaint) about the imitation.

!

Key Takeaway: When a paragraph has no clear topic sentence, you must read the whole passage and make notes about its main idea. Then check which heading best summarises that idea.

📝
Task: The reading passage has six paragraphs, A–F. Choose the correct heading for each paragraph from the list of headings below. Write the correct number, i–viii.

CAHOKIA — ANCESTOR OF TODAY'S CAPITAL CITIES

Paragraph A

A thousand years ago the Mississippians, a diverse group of Native Americans who lived in the area which is today known as the south-eastern United States, took a small village on the Mississippi River and turned it into one of the world's first great urban centres. Cahokia, as it has been called by archaeologists, became as large as London was in the 11th century, and some would argue that it was just as forward-looking and prosperous as its European equivalents. Sophisticated, cosmopolitan, and ahead of its time, Cahokia was at the heart of ancient society in North America; an ancestor of today's capital cities.

Paragraph B

In one respect in particular, Cahokia was quite unusual compared to other cities around at the same time. Archaeologists working on the site have found enough evidence over the past fifty years to conclude that, at a certain time, around 35% of the population were not from Cahokia at all; it seems that many of the tribes that lived all along the Mississippi River at some point began to relocate to Cahokia. These researchers have been unable to find more than a handful of other examples of such relocation of tribes, but they do know that something about Cahokia attracted thousands of people to this regional centre. And that, they postulated, appears to have been thanks to a small group of planners who one day decided to redesign the entire village.

Paragraph C

After the redesigns of the village were put in place, the Native Americans at Cahokia worked with tireless determination to carry them out. Over the course of a few decades, they transported huge volumes of soil from the nearby countryside to create 120 huge mounds of earth, the biggest of which rose to one hundred feet. On top of these, they built a vast urban environment, complete with a vibrant town centre, municipal buildings, and a fifty-acre plaza at the foot of the biggest mound. What makes it even more impressive to our modern imaginations is that, with no machinery then, they used their bare hands and woven baskets to dig up and carry the soil from the surrounding regions back to their city-in-waiting. Eventually, after these efforts, the vision of the city planners was fulfilled, but even they could not have predicted how popular Cahokia would become.

Paragraph D

From this period on, Cahokia was alive with intense activity, and grew in size every year, partly because of the co-operation between the residents. While the men busied themselves with manual work, like constructing new buildings, or hunting and fishing in the forests and rivers within a day's walk of the city, the women made sure that the fields stayed healthy and grew crops, and the homes were kept clean. In many ways, it seems to have been the ideal place to live, and one with an exciting and prosperous future ahead of it. And yet, having become a major population centre around AD 1050, by 1350 it had been almost completely abandoned. Somewhere in the course of 300 years, something happened to Cahokia to cause this, but it is an enigma that even archaeologists or historians themselves struggle to resolve.

Paragraph E

This rather curious state of affairs exists today because researchers have never found a single piece of evidence that can conclusively explain why the residents left. Academics who have studied other Native American sites have always found weapons of war buried deep underground. And yet, the bows, arrows and swords that littered the ground at these other sites were nowhere to be seen at Cahokia. Other factors, such as disease or colonisation from European invasion, do not seem to be possible in this case, as common as they were elsewhere at that time. The absence of definitive theories as to Cahokia's decline is highly unusual, but then again, Cahokia was no ordinary city and perhaps comparisons with other urban centres of the time cannot be made.

Paragraph F

While academics remain bemused as to why the residents fled the city, we can still marvel at the individual artefacts that archaeologists have discovered: the jewellery worn, the pots used to cook in, the small workshop at the base of one of the mounds. That said, there is also a more unpleasant side to their investigations. Human sacrifice, it seems, was a common fact of life in Cahokia; even if we cannot be sure whether this was for religious or for other reasons, we can have no doubt that it happened frequently. The bodies of hundreds of people, mostly young women, have been found buried in mass graves, and the way in which they died was often horrific. A sombre reminder that even 'advanced' city states had their shadowy sides.

Skill Focus: Task Achievement (TA)

Avoid the most common mistakes that lower your TA score
⚠️
Task Achievement is the mark you get for how well you answer the question. Common mistakes that prevent a high TA score:
❌ Not including an overview statement
❌ Misreporting data
❌ Not highlighting key information or trends
❌ Speculating or giving opinions about WHY
❌ Using an inappropriate (informal) tone
❌ Writing fewer than 150 words
❌ Not including a summary/conclusion
❌ Not including enough data

Practice: Analysing a Line Graph

The line graph shows reasons people gave for moving away from a capital city to the countryside (1990–2010).

Rising cost of living Traffic Lifestyle

Model Answer Analysis

Read this answer and match the teacher's comments (A–D) to the numbered sections:

The line graph sets out the main motivations people expressed for relocating to the countryside from the city in the years 1990, 2000 and 2010. The overall trend for the period is of an increase in the numbers moving away from the city.

According to the graph, the main reason for relocation was the rising cost of living. In 1990, 65,000 people left the city because of this, then 85,000 left in 2000, then 80,000 people moved away in 2010, so it actually increased by 20,000 initially (between 1990 and 2000), before going down by 5,000 in those last ten years between 2000 and 2010. (1)

It was a different story for traffic and lifestyle. Both had the same number of city leavers at the start of the period. Subsequently, both categories saw increases, with traffic first going up by a large number between 1990 and 2000 and then even more steeply after that. Lifestyle leavers rose consistently over the whole period, going up to 30,000 initially, then up again to 2010. (3)

So, what does it all mean? For me, the answer is crystal clear. Overall, the graph shows that a huge number of people moved away from the city to the countryside in a twenty-year period. (4)

Match the comments to sections (1), (3), (4):

  • A: Don't speculate – just report what you see on the graph.
  • B: This section is too mechanical – avoid simply listing changes to a single category. Focus on highlighting key trends.
  • C: You've included a conclusion, but it doesn't summarise key features. Your tone is too informal.
  • D: This section makes its points more clearly but fails to include key data to demonstrate them.

Section (1): B — Too mechanical, just listing data for one category.

Section (3): D — Makes good comparative points but lacks specific data.

Section (4): C — Has a conclusion but doesn't summarise trends; tone is informal ("crystal clear", "For me").

T

Summary/Conclusion Tip: Don't include data in your conclusion – it's a summary of the overall trends. Also avoid phrases like "For me..." or "I think..." Keep the tone formal and objective.

Best Conclusion Example

Option B (Best): "Overall, the graph suggests the number of people relocating to the countryside rose across the period. Cost of living was the main reason for relocation by some distance, despite a fall in numbers in the second half of the period. Traffic saw the greatest overall increase, with lifestyle seeing a relatively slow but steady rise."

This summarises all three trends without repeating specific data. It's formal, objective, and comprehensive.

Exam Skills: Writing Task 1

Full exam-style Writing Task 1 practice with model answer
⏱
You should spend about 20 minutes on this task.
The line chart below shows the results of a survey giving the reasons why people moved to the capital city of a particular country.
Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.
Write at least 150 words.

Survey results: reasons for moving to capital city

Employment Study Family/friends Adventure

Writing Framework

Paragraph 1: Introduction — paraphrase the task question
Paragraph 2: Overview — 2–3 key trends (no specific numbers)
Paragraph 3: Detail body 1 — describe the two highest categories with data
Paragraph 4: Detail body 2 — describe the remaining categories with data

The line chart illustrates the results of a survey on the reasons why people relocated to the capital city of a particular country between 2000 and 2015.

Overall, employment and study were by far the most significant reasons for moving, both showing substantial increases over the period. In contrast, family/friends and adventure remained comparatively minor factors throughout.

In 2000, employment was the leading reason, with approximately 50,000 people citing it, and this figure rose steadily to a peak of around 90,000 in 2010 before declining slightly to 80,000 by 2015. Study, meanwhile, saw the most dramatic growth of all categories: starting at just 10,000 in 2000, it climbed gradually to 20,000 in 2005, then surged to 50,000 in 2010 and reached approximately 90,000 in 2015, overtaking employment by the end of the period.

By contrast, family/friends and adventure both remained relatively stable. The former held steady at around 10,000 until 2010 before experiencing a modest rise to 20,000 in 2015, while adventure showed a similarly flat trend, hovering between 5,000 and 10,000 across the entire period.

Error Correction

Find and correct the errors in these Task 1 sentences:

  1. "The graph shows that people are leaving the city in 1990 because of the high cost."
  2. "I think the main reason for the decrease was probably because people couldn't afford it."

Skill Focus: Understanding Paraphrase

Recognise how information is restated in different words
💡
Many IELTS listening tasks test your ability to recognise paraphrases or synonyms. The key ideas in a question are expressed in different words in the recording. However, the answers you write must be exactly as you hear them — and must be spelt correctly.

Paraphrase Examples from Museum of London Life

Written Text (Brochure) Spoken Paraphrase (Guide)
"thrilling journey from ancient times to modern-day life and beyond""an exciting trip through history from prehistoric times to the present and future"
"how prehistoric humans used to live""the way early people lived thousands of years ago"
"when London was just open countryside""before London became a city, when it was all fields and forests"
"surrounded by fascinating exhibits – images, photos, maps and all kinds of objects from years gone by""you'll see displays with pictures, photographs, maps and artefacts from the past"
"transported into the final era – the 22nd century""taken forward to what London might look like in the next hundred years"
T

Key Principle: The question paper uses one set of words. The recording uses different words with the same meaning. Your answer must be the exact words from the recording.

Skill Focus: Form Completion

Section 1 strategies for filling in forms accurately
⚠️
Section 1 is the least difficult part of the Listening test and is often a form completion task. If you're aiming for a high score, listen carefully from the very first moment — try to get all 10 marks, as each question carries one mark, the same as harder sections.

Strategy: Before You Listen

🔍 Study the form

Look at the labels (Name, Address, etc.) to predict what type of information you need.

🔢 Check word limits

"NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS AND/OR A NUMBER" means exactly that. Exceeding it = wrong.

📄 Read surrounding info

Information already given on the form helps you follow the conversation.

✏️ Predict answer types

Is it a name? A number? A date? A place? This helps you listen for the right thing.

Skill Focus: Names & Numbers

How different types of information are spoken in English

Knowing the conventions for expressing information in English helps you avoid mistakes. Study these patterns:

Type Written Form Spoken As You Would NOT Hear
Phone number 410266 "four, one, zero, [pause] two, six, six" OR "...two, double six" "four, one, oh, two, double six" (oh not used for zero in UK phone numbers consistently)
Century 18th century "the eighteenth century" "century eighteen"
Year 2012 "twenty twelve" OR "two thousand and twelve" "two zero twelve"
Date 20/7/76 "the twentieth of July, nineteen seventy-six" "twenty, seven, seventy-six"
Hyphenated name Baker-Jones "B-A-K-E-R, hyphen, J-O-N-E-S" "B-A-K-E-R, line, J-O-N-E-S"
Price £5.40 "five pounds forty" OR "five forty" "five pounds forty pence" (unusual in casual speech)
Email museuminfo@history.org "museuminfo [one word] at history dot org" "history point org" (point not used, dot is standard)
Time 09:00 "nine A-M" OR "nine in the morning" "oh-nine A-M" (unusual)

🔐 Remember These Conventions

  • Numbers: money, dates, years, telephone numbers
  • Spelling: names of people, places, addresses, email addresses
  • Problem sounds: Pay attention to letters that sound similar in your language (e.g., B/V, L/R, P/B)

Skill Focus: Multiple Choice

Strategy for tackling multiple choice questions in Listening
💡
For multiple-choice tasks, you will normally hear all three options mentioned in the recording in some way, but only one will answer the question. The correct answer is often a paraphrase of what the speaker says.

Strategy

1

Read the stem carefully

Identify the key words that might be paraphrased

2

Think about paraphrases

How could each option be said differently?

3

Don't jump to conclusions

The speaker may mention and then reject an option

T

Exam Tip: Don't write down the first piece of information that seems to fit the gap. Sometimes in the Listening test, the speaker will talk for longer than you expect before the correct answer becomes clear. A speaker may seem to confirm an answer and then change their mind.

First conditional structures commonly heard in Listening Section 1
G

The Rule: Future time conditionals follow the first conditional structure:
If + present simple, ... will + bare infinitive

Time Conjunctions That Work Like "If"

Conjunction Meaning Example
as long ason the condition thatYou can get a discounted ticket as long as you book online.

as soon asimmediately whenYour bus will depart as soon as everyone boards.

The 4 Assessment Criteria

FC — Fluency & Coherence

How smoothly you speak, with minimal hesitation. How well you connect and organise your ideas using linking phrases.

LR — Lexical Resource

The range and accuracy of your vocabulary. Use of collocations, idioms, and less common words appropriately.

GRA — Grammatical Range & Accuracy

Using a variety of grammatical structures correctly. Complex sentences, conditionals, passive voice, etc.

P — Pronunciation

Clarity, stress, intonation, and rhythm. Being easy to understand at all times. Natural-sounding connected speech.

1

Speaking Test — Part 1

Simple general questions about familiar topics (4–5 minutes)
💡
Part 1 questions focus on general familiar topics that reflect your everyday life, rather than complex or abstract questions. Give answers that are 2–3 sentences long — not too short, not too long.

Sample Part 1 Questions (Urban & Rural Life)

  1. Do you live in a house or an apartment?
  2. If you could choose any country to visit, where would you go?
  3. What are the main issues affecting life in your town?
  4. What do you like about the area where you live?
  5. ❌ How might overpopulation affect city life in the future? (Too complex for Part 1)
  6. How often do you use public transport in your town/city?
  7. ❌ Why is it important to look after places of natural beauty? (Too abstract for Part 1)
  8. Do you often visit parks in your town/city?
T

Common Mistakes in Part 1:

  • Giving one-word answers (not enough language to assess)
  • Going off-topic or rambling for too long
  • Using memorised scripts (examiners can tell!)
2

Speaking Test — Part 2 (Long Turn)

Speak for 1–2 minutes on a topic card (3–4 minutes total)

Which Task Card is Correct for IELTS?

✅ Card A (Correct Format)

Describe a rural town that you plan to visit in the future.

You should say:

  • where the town is
  • when you would like to go
  • who you would go with

and explain what you would like to do there.

❌ Card B (Incorrect)

Describe this town in a rural area.

Problem: Uses a photo — real IELTS Part 2 never includes images. Also too vague.

T

Part 2 Tips:

  • You do NOT need to cover all four prompts equally — some will be easier to expand on
  • Use your 1-minute preparation time to make notes (not full sentences)
  • Aim to vary verb forms and grammar structures
  • Use synonyms and paraphrase to show vocabulary range
  • Never memorise answers — examiners can tell and it will affect your score

3

Speaking Test — Part 3

Two-way discussion about abstract ideas related to the Part 2 topic (4–5 minutes)

What Type of Question is Being Asked?

Part 3 questions ask you to perform different functions. Understanding the function helps you decide what language to use:

Function Example Question Useful Language
Make a prediction How might overpopulation affect city life? I would imagine that... / It's likely that... / In all probability...
Compare & contrast Which is preferable: a city apartment or a countryside house? On one hand... / While... / In contrast... / Having said that...
Hypothetical situation If private cars were banned from cities, what might happen? If that were to happen... / Supposing... / That might sound ridiculous, but...
Cause & effect Why do people decide to move from the city to the countryside? One of the main reasons is... / This is largely due to... / As a result of...
Analyse past & present How was family life different before transport linked towns and cities? In the past... / Traditionally... / Nowadays... / Things have changed because...

Assessment Criteria in Action

Read these comments about a candidate's Part 3 performance and match each to the correct criterion: FC (Fluency & Coherence), LR (Lexical Resource), GRA (Grammatical Range & Accuracy), or P (Pronunciation).

  1. Uses stress and intonation appropriately, e.g. NOT just NAtionally, but INternationally.
  2. Uses a good range of more advanced structures naturally, e.g. if you were just visiting the city, it probably wouldn't bother you...
  3. Speaks fluently with very rare hesitation or repetition.
  4. Avoids making grammatical mistakes.
  5. Uses varied vocabulary that is relevant to the topics discussed.
  6. Develops the topics fully and appropriately; supplies answers of an appropriate length.
  7. Every answer can be understood very easily.
  8. Uses less common vocabulary, including collocation and idiomatic phrases, e.g. the bright lights of the big city | traditional family unit
  9. Uses appropriate phrases to introduce and connect ideas, e.g. Generally speaking, though... | Having said that...
  1. P — Pronunciation (stress and intonation)
  2. GRA — Grammatical Range and Accuracy (advanced structures)
  3. FC — Fluency and Coherence (smooth delivery)
  4. GRA — Grammatical Range and Accuracy (accuracy)
  5. LR — Lexical Resource (varied, relevant vocabulary)
  6. FC — Fluency and Coherence (topic development)
  7. P — Pronunciation (intelligibility)
  8. LR — Lexical Resource (collocations, idioms)
  9. FC — Fluency and Coherence (discourse markers)
